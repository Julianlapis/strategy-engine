# Value Architecture

> Source: Alex M H Smith, BasicArts. "Strategy Secrets" / "The No Bullshit Strategy Cheatsheet"
> Use as a thinking lens, not a deliverable. The strategy serves the brief, not this framework.

## When to Use

- Testing whether a strategy names what kind of value the brand creates (narrative, brief, product)
- Pressure-testing whether value differentiation is structural or cosmetic (courtroom, pressure-test)
- Evaluating whether a product strategy is new or repositioned sameness (product)
- Checking whether execution matches the value type claimed (narrative, brief)

---

## The Core Idea

All businesses are systems designed to produce value and exchange it for cash. Strategy is: "your way of maximizing the value you produce." Not describing what you do. Not listing features. Maximizing the value people receive.

People don't buy what you do (product). People buy what you do for them (value). Great strategy is about maximizing that second thing.

## Two Characteristics of Great Strategy

1. Give people something they want (that they can't get elsewhere)
2. Do it in a way that's hard for competitors to copy

If a strategy doesn't pass both tests, it's not a strategy. It's an aspiration.

---

## The 4 Types of Value

Every business creates value in one of four ways. Knowing which type you're playing clarifies the entire strategic posture.

### 1. Generic offer for niche audience
Standard product, tailored to a specific group, producing enhanced value for them.
- Example: Diamond car insurance for women
- Strategic posture: audience intimacy, not product innovation

### 2. Niche offer for broad audience
The offer and product are specific, but the audience is anyone who needs that thing.
- Example: Airbnb ("feel like a local when abroad")
- Strategic posture: product distinctiveness, universal need

### 3. Stripped back service at low cost
Over-served market. Remove elements to lower cost.
- Example: Ryanair, Costco
- Strategic posture: subtraction as strategy, not degradation

### 4. Pumped up service at high cost
Under-served market. Add elements to the experience and charge premium.
- Example: Four Seasons, Louis Vuitton
- Strategic posture: elevation, not decoration

**The diagnostic question:** Which of these four is our brand playing? If you can't answer cleanly, the strategy hasn't made a choice.

## 3 Techniques for Creating New Value

These create surprising value where none existed. Not incremental improvement, but category-shifting moves.

### 1. Context Shift
Take your existing product and position it to compete with something unexpected (where it could also do a job).
- Example: Halo Top positioned low-calorie ice cream as a post-workout snack, not a dessert
- The move: change the competitive frame, not the product

### 2. Unexpected Value
Import a form of value that's common in another category but unknown in yours.
- Example: Lush brought "freshness" from food into cosmetics
- The move: cross-category theft, applied with conviction

### 3. Contrarian Value
Do the opposite of competitors and see what new value this creates.
- Example: Nintendo lowered console performance to introduce portability with the Switch
- The move: invert the category assumption, find what falls out

## Execute With Power

Identifying new value isn't enough. Execution requires force and deliberateness. Four moves:

1. **Create at least one fresh innovation** dedicated to delivering the value
2. **Remove everything** that softens or contradicts it
3. **Rebrand** to draw attention to it in a compelling way
4. **Shape team roles** around it

The world's greatest companies win not because their ideas are clever, but because they go all-out in delivering them.

## How to Judge a Strategy

### The Best Test
Remove all value judgment wording ("best," "leading," "superior"). Ask: "Would my competitor's customers switch to us based on what remains?"

### The Supreme Test
Describe the strategy to a colleague. Do they look impressed and a bit threatened? If they shrug, the strategy doesn't create real value.

### Make Your Strategy Scan
1. It must be a real goal, not a platitude. If it needs no action, it's not a goal.
2. Admit prior deficiency. If you were already doing it, you wouldn't need the strategy.
3. A strategy that a colleague calls "a culture, not a goal" is not a strategy. It's a vibe.

---

## How to Execute (Only 2 Levers)

There are only two ways a strategy comes to life:

1. **Product:** the practical things you change
2. **Communication:** how you tell people about it

That's it. Every strategic action falls into one of these two buckets. If you can't point to a product change or a communication change, the strategy hasn't been executed.

---

## The Absolute Worst Way to Give Value

Being "better." Doing the same thing as your competition but trying to do it a little better. Bad strategy masquerades as good strategy by claiming superiority without structural difference. Only is better than best.
