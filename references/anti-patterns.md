# Strategy Anti-Patterns

Failures specific to AI-assisted strategy writing. These are the traps that make strategy output feel like it came from a machine instead of a strategist. Guard against all of them.

---

## 1. The Generic Cultural Take

**What it looks like:** "Gen Z values authenticity and craves real connection in a digital world."

**Why it fails:** This could appear in any deck for any brand in any category. It tells the client something they already know (or worse, something they've heard so many times it registers as noise). Cultural observations become insights only when they're specific enough to suggest an action for THIS brand.

**The fix:** Name the specific cultural signal. A tweet with engagement numbers. A behavior shift with a date. A subculture with a name. A tension between two specific groups. "Women are romanticizing their solo lives on TikTok because dating apps killed the feeling of romance" is specific. "People want more authentic experiences" is not.

**Quick test:** Could you paste this cultural take into a deck for a different brand and have it still work? If yes, it's too generic.

---

## 2. Strategy Without Tension

**What it looks like:** A deck that goes Challenge → Insight → Strategy without making the reader feel the problem. The strategy arrives before the argument earns it.

**Why it fails:** Persuasion requires tension. The reader needs to sit in the discomfort of the problem before they're ready to accept a solution. A strategy that arrives too early feels arbitrary. A strategy that resolves a felt tension feels inevitable.

**The fix:** Spend more time on the problem. Make it vivid. Use cultural evidence, audience quotes, competitive gaps, and emotional language to make the reader feel stuck before you offer a way out. The Bumble deck spends 6 slides building the problem (dating app fatigue, romance fading, algorithmic world erasing mystery) before introducing the concept.

**Quick test:** If you deleted the strategy section, would the problem section alone be interesting? If not, the tension isn't strong enough.

## 3. Consultant-Speak

**What it looks like:** "We recommend a 360 omnichannel experience ecosystem that creates meaningful touchpoints across the consumer journey to drive brand equity and purchase consideration."

**Why it fails:** Every word is technically correct and collectively meaningless. This is the language of people who are paid by the hour, not the insight. Strategy should feel like a sharp person talking to you across a table, not a management consulting firm justifying its retainer.

**The fix:** Say the specific thing. "Run 4 pop-ups in Brooklyn, each themed around a different dimension of romance, promoted entirely through Bumble's own user base." That's a strategy. "Create immersive brand experiences" is not.

**Quick test:** Read it aloud. Would you say this in a bar to a smart friend? If not, rewrite it.

---

## 4. The Insight That Isn't

**What it looks like:** "Consumers are spending more time on mobile devices" presented as an insight.

**Why it fails:** Observations describe what's happening. Insights reveal WHY it's happening and what it means for the brand. An insight contains a tension. An observation contains a fact.

**The hierarchy:**
- **Data point:** "67% of women deleted a dating app in the last year"
- **Observation:** "Dating app fatigue is widespread"
- **Insight:** "Dating apps turned the pathway to romance into a chore, and women are searching for that romantic feeling in the rest of their lives instead"
- **Strategic insight:** "Bumble's opportunity is to own romance as a lifestyle, not just dating as a function"

**Quick test:** Does the insight suggest a specific action? Does it reveal a tension? If it's just a fact about the world, it's an observation.

---

## 5. Strategy by Adjective

**What it looks like:** "The brand should be bold, authentic, innovative, and human-centered."

**Why it fails:** Adjectives aren't strategy. Every brand wants to be bold and authentic. Strategy is choosing what to do (and what NOT to do). A list of adjectives makes no choices and forecloses nothing.

**The fix:** Replace adjectives with decisions. "Bumble becomes the champion of feminine romance's future" is a strategic choice. It forecloses Bumble being a hookup app, a friend-finder, or a gender-neutral platform. It chooses.

**Quick test:** Does the strategy say no to anything? If it could be endorsed by every brand in the category, it's not a strategy.

---

## 6. The False Bridge

**What it looks like:** A "From → To" slide that doesn't earn the bridge. "From product to experience" without explaining why or how.

**Why it fails:** Transformation language is easy to write and hard to substantiate. Anyone can draw an arrow between two words. The bridge needs to be supported by the insight, the cultural truth, and the competitive reality. Otherwise it's aspiration without argument.

**The fix:** Every "From → To" must be earned by the preceding sections. The reader should be able to trace the logic: this cultural truth + this brand DNA + this insight = this transformation is the only move.

---

## 7. Mood Board as Strategy

**What it looks like:** Beautiful visual references and cultural collages presented as strategic thinking.

**Why it fails:** Aesthetic direction is expression, not strategy. A mood board tells you how something feels but not why it exists, who it's for, or what business problem it solves. Expression follows strategy. If the strategy changes, the mood board should change with it.

**The fix:** Every visual reference should trace back to a strategic decision. "We use this visual language because our audience processes romance through cinematic and literary aesthetics, not dating app UI" connects expression to insight. "Here's a pretty mood board" does not.

---

## 8. The Recycled Framework

**What it looks like:** Applying a pre-existing framework (Jobs to Be Done, Blue Ocean, Brand Key, etc.) without adaptation. The brand's reality gets squeezed into the framework's boxes.

**Why it fails:** Frameworks are thinking tools, not outputs. When the framework becomes the deliverable, the strategy serves the framework instead of the brief. The client hired you for your thinking, not for a fill-in-the-blank template.

**The fix:** Use frameworks to generate thinking, then discard them. The output should be a narrative that feels custom to this brand and this moment, even if a framework helped you get there. Your narrative engines (Brand DNA vs Cultural Truth, False Choice → Reframe, etc.) are flexible structures, not rigid templates. They adapt to each brief.
